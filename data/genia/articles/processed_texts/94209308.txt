Characterization of the Human Gene Encoding LBR , an Integral Protein of the Nuclear Envelope Inner Membrane* ( Received for publication , November 1 , 1993 , and in revised form , January 7 , 1994 ) Ekkehard Schuler } , Feng Lin , and Howard J. Worman } From the Department of Medicine and Brookdale Center for Molecular Biology , Mount Sinai School of Medicine , New York , New York 10029 We have characterized the human gene encoding LBR , an integral protein of the nuclear envelope inner membrane .
Restriction mapping shows that the transcription unit spans approximately 35 kilobases .
A transcription start site is located approximately 4 kilobases 5 to the translation initiation codon , and an RNA splice of 3863 bases occurs in the 5'-untranslated region to generate mature HeLa cell mRNA .
5 to the identified transcription start site are two CCAAT sequences and potential recognition sites for several transcription factors including Spl , AP-1 , AP-2 , and NF-kB .
There are 13 protein coding exons in the LBR gene .
LBR 's nucleoplasmic domain is encoded by exons 1-4 , and its hydrophobic domain , with eight putative transmembrane segments , is encoded by exons 5-13 .
The hydrophobic domain is homologous to three yeast polypeptides , suggesting that this higher eukaryotic gene could have evolved from recombination between a gene that encoded a soluble nuclear protein and a membrane protein gene similar to those in yeast .
These results are the first to demonstrate the structural organization of a vertebrate gene encoding an integral membrane protein of the nuclear envelope that may be a member of a family of polypeptides conserved in evolution .
The nuclear envelope is composed of the nuclear lamina , the nuclear pore complexes , and the nuclear membranes ( Nigg , 1989 ; Wiese and Wilson , 1993 ) .
The nuclear membranes can be divided into three morphologically distinct but interconnected domains : the outer nuclear membrane , the inner nuclear membrane , and the nuclear pore membrane .
The inner nuclear membrane is adjacent to the nuclear lamina ( Fawcett , 1966 ) , a meshwork of intermediate filament proteins termed lamins ( Aebi et al .
, 1986 ; Fisher et al .
, 1986 ; McKeon et al .
, 1986 ) .
The nuclear lamina is a discontinuous structure that occupies only a fraction of the nuclear periphery ( Paddy et al .
, 1990 ) , and at some points , the inner nuclear membrane may interact directly with the chromatin .

The first to be identified was an avian protein called the lamin B receptor , or LBR , that binds in vitro to lamin B ( Worman et al .
, 1988 ) .
Subsequently , a mammalian homolog of avian LBR was identified by cross-reactive autoantibodies from patients with primary biliary cirrhosis ( Courvalin et al .
, 1990 ) .
Chicken and human LBR are polytopic proteins with basic nucleoplasmic amino-terminal domains and hydrophobic domains with eight putative transmembrane segments ( Worman et al .
, 1990 ; Ye and Worman , 1994 ) .
The amino-terminal domain binds to lamin B and DNA and may be involved in attaching the lamina and the chromatin to the inner nuclear membrane in cells ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) .
LBR 's overall primary structure is not homologous to other identified higher eukaryotic proteins ; however , three yeast proteins have been identified that are homologous to the hydrophobic domain of LBR but lack long , basic amino-terminal domains ( Chen et al .
, 1991 ; Lorenz and Parks , 1992 ; Shimanuki et al .
, 1992 ) .
Characterization of a vertebrate gene encoding LBR could help establish its relationship to other membrane proteins and provide clues concerning the evolution of the higher eukaryotic nucleus .
Cloning and sequence analysis of the regulatory regions of the LBR gene would also provide reagents and information necessary to examine the expression of a major structural protein of the nuclear envelope in differentiation , development , and carcinogenesis .
For these reasons , we have determined the structural organization and promoter sequence of the human LBR gene .

Subcloning and DNA Sequencing-Fragments of A clones generated by restriction endonuclease digestion were subcloned into PUC-19 ( Life Technologies , Inc. ) by standard methods ( Sambrook et al .
, 1989 ) .
DNA sequencing was performed by the dideoxy chain termination method ( Sanger et al .

Custom oligonucleotide primers for sequencing were obtained from Genset ( Paris , France ) .
Both DNA strands were sequenced with dGTP , and dITP was also used in regions that demonstrated compressions or were GC-rich .
HeLa cell cDNA clone QY-1 ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) was sequenced concurrently for comparison .
DNA Sequence Analysis-Initial DNA sequence analysis was performed on Macintosh and Powerbook computers ( Apple Computer , Cu-pertino , CA ) using version 1.0 of the DNA Strider `` software package ( Commissariat a l'Energie Atomique-France ) .
Additional sequence analysis was performed on a Macintosh microcomputer using the MacVector software package ( IBI-Kodak , New Haven , CT ) and on the computer facilities of the Department of Biomathematics of the Mount Sinai School of Medicine ( New York , NY ) using the GCG ( version 7.B ) sequence analysis software package ( Genetics Computer Group , Inc. , Madison , WI ) ( Devereux et al .
, 1984 ) .
Sequence alignments were performed with the GCG package and also the DNA Translator and Alignment program ( Eernisse , 1992 ) .
Estimation of Sizes of Partially Sequenced Introns-Sizes of large partially sequenced introns were estimated by analysis of restriction maps .
Sizes of partially sequenced introns less than 2 kilobases were estimated by performing the polymerase chain reaction with primers complementary to flanking exon sequences to amplify the intronic DNA .
The polymerase chain reaction was performed as described ( Saiki et al .
, 1987 ) using the GeneAmp kit ( Perkin-Elmer ) and the following param-eters : denaturation at 94 °C for 1 min , annealing at 50 °C for 2 min , and extension at 72 °C for 3 min for 25 cycles .
A DNA or subcloned plasmid DNA was used as a template .
Amplified products were electrophoresed on agarose gels and their migrations compared with the migrations of DNA fragments of known sizes .
Primer Extension Analysis-An oligonucleotide of sequence 5'-CCAGGTTCCGGCGGTGAC-3 ' ( Genset ) , reverse complementary to the human LBR gene from nucleotide +65 to +83 , was P-labeled with T4 polynucleotide kinase and used for primer extension with total HeLa cell RNA .
RNA isolation and primer extension analysis were performed as described ( Sambrook et al .
, 1989 ) .
Reverse transcriptase was obtained from Promega Corp. ( Madison , WI ) .
The position of the transcription start site was determined by comparing the electrophoretic migration of the primer extension product with the migrations of the products of a DNA sequencing reaction using an LBR genomic subclone and the same primer .

RESULTS Isolation , Restriction Mapping , and Characterization of Human LBR Genomic Clones-Approximately 1 x 10° recombinant clones of a A DASH human lymphocyte genomic library were screened using human LBR cDNA clone QY-1 ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) .
In this screening , 13 hybridizing clones were isolated that contained the protein coding exons and introns of the LBR gene except for exon 5 and its contiguous intronic sequences .
The same library was therefore screened again with both a 3.5-kilobase HindIII restriction fragment corresponding to a portion of intron 4 and a polymerase chain reaction-gen-erated probe of 200 nucleotides corresponding to exon 5 .
Eleven clones were isolated with the intron 4-specific probe but none with the exon 5-specific probe .
We therefore screened approximately 1 x 10° recombinant clones of another genomic library ( A EMBL3A from human placenta ) with the exon 5-specific probe and isolated six hybridizing clones .
To obtain the DNA for all of intron 4 , 1 x 10° recombinants clones of the EMBL3A library were screened again with the upstream-most BamHUHindIII fragment of A clone X to obtain A clone D ( Fig .
1a ) .
Eight of the overlapping A clones containing the entire gene were analyzed by restriction mapping ( Fig .
1a ) and Southern hybridization of overlapping restriction fragments ( data not shown ) .
Fragments of these A clones were subcloned into PUC-19 for sequencing ( Fig .
16 ) .
From these data , the structural organization of the human LBR gene was determined ( Fig .
1¢ ) .
The Human LBR Gene-The partial nucleotide sequence of the human LBR gene is shown in Fig .
2 .
The transcription unit spans approximately 35 kilobases .
Comparison of the LBR genomic sequence with that of HeLa cell cDNA ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) shows that the genomic DNA encoding the 5'-untranslated sequence is interrupted by a 3863-nucleotide segment .
This segment contains intron-exon splicing consensus sequences ( Mount , 1982 ) at its 5'- and 3'-ends and is spliced in the generation of HeLa LBR mRNA .
Primer extension analysis using HeLa cell RNA identifies a transcription start site 88 nucleotides upstream from the 5'-splice junction of this segment and 3951 nucleotides 5 ' to the translation initiation codon .
The region 5 ' to the identified transcription start site is GC-rich and contains two possible CCAAT boxes and potential recognition sequences for several transcription factors ( Fig .
2 ) .
The putative CCAAT boxes are 122 and 162 nucleotides upstream from the identified transcription start site .
Five Sp-1 , 1 AP-1 , 2 AP-2 , and 1 NF-kB consensus sequences are within 1000 nucleotides of the transcription start site .
There is no TATA-like element in this region .
The human LBR gene contains 13 protein coding exons .
The positions of the introns relative to LBR 's protein structural domains are shown in Fig .
3 .
The first 4 exons encode the nucleoplasmic amino-terminal domain plus the first five amino acids of the first putative transmembrane segment .
The DNA binding domain of LBR , located between amino acids 71 and 100 ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) , is encoded by exon 2 .
Exons 5-13 encode the protein 's hydrophobic domain that contains eight putative transmembrane segments .
The 12 introns that interrupt the protein-coding region of the LBR gene range in size from 99 bases to approximately 10 kilobases ( Table I ) .

DISCUSSION In the present study , we have determined the structural organization of the human gene encoding LBR .
LBR is an integral protein of the nuclear envelope inner membrane that has been shown to interact with lamin B and DNA ( Worman et al .
, 1988 ; Ye and Worman , 1994 ) .
Besides three vertebrate ( Doring and Stick , 1990 ; Zewe et al .
, 1991 ; Lin and Worman , 1993 ) and one invertebrate ( Osman et al .
, 1990 ) nuclear lamin genes , other higher eukaryotic genes encoding nuclear envelope proteins have not to our knowledge been characterized .
In addition to LBR , several integral proteins of the inner nuclear membrane have been identified ( Senior and Gerace , 1988 ; Harel et al .
, 1989 ; Bailer et al .
, 1991 ; Padan et al .
, 1990 ; Foisner and Gerace , 1993 ) .
An integral protein has been characterized in the inner nuclear membrane of avian cells that cross-reacts with anti-LBR antibodies ( Bailer et al .
, 1991 ) .
This protein is either the same as or highly related to LBR .
Four other inner nuclear membrane proteins have been described in mammals ( Senior and Gerace , 1988 ; Foisner and Gerace , 1993 ) , but detailed information regarding their cross-reactivity with anti-LBR antibodies , their primary structures , and their genomic sequences is lacking .
Any possible relationships between the genes encoding these four proteins and the LBR gene can not be established at this time .
The primary structure of otefin , an integral protein of the inner nuclear membrane of Drosophila , differs significantly from that of LBR ( Padan et al .
, 1990 ) .
It has been proposed that the shuffling of exons that encode functional polypeptide domains and recombination within introns occur during the evolution of higher eukaryotic genes ( Gilbert , 1978 , 1985 ; Tonegawa et al .
, 1978 ) .
In the human LBR gene , exons 1-4 encode polypeptide domains with nuclear lo-calization , lamin binding , and DNA binding functions .
Exons 5-13 encode hydrophobic polypeptide segments that can function as transmembrane domains .
These features suggest that the LBR gene may have evolved from recombination between two primordial genes , linking exons 1-4 with exons 5-13 .

sts1* gene of S. pombe encodes a protein homologous to LBKR 's hydrophobic domain without a basic amino-terminal domain ( Shimanuki et al .
, 1992 ) .
The sts1* gene product has not been localized to the nuclear envelope and , based on its putative function , more likely resides in either the endoplasmic reticulum , vacuolar membrane , or plasma membrane ( Shimanuki et al .
, 1992 ) .
Two genes of S. cerevisiae ( Chen et al .
, 1991 ; Lorenz and Parks , 1992 ) have also been identified that encode polypeptides homologous to the hydrophobic domain of vertebrate LBR but lack LBR-like amino-terminal domains .
One of these , the ERG24 gene , encodes a sterol C-14 reductase that would presumably be located in the endoplasmic reticulum ( Lorenz and Parks , 1992 ) .
Although speculative , it can be hypothesized that the first 4 exons of the LBR gene arose from an ancestral gene that encoded a soluble nuclear protein and that exons 5-13 evolved from another ancestral gene , similar to those now present in yeast , that encoded a polytopic protein of the endoplasmic reticulum , vacuolar membrane , or plasma membrane .
Along these lines , the hydrophobic domain of LBR , encoded by exons 5-13 , is retained in the endoplasmic reticulum when expressed in transfected cells ( Soullam and Worman , 1993 ) .
The amino-terminal domain of LBR , encoded by exons 1-4 , is transported to the nucleus in transfected cells ( Soullam and Worman , 1993 ; Smith and Blobel , 1993 ) .
The ability of a putative primordial integral membrane protein to be targeted to the inner nuclear membrane may have therefore evolved from a genetic recombination that brought LBR exons 1-4 together with exons 5-13 .
It is not clear if LBR is functionally related to the three homologous yeast proteins .
Because these yeast proteins do not have LBR-like amino termini , they do not likely bind to lamins or DNA ( Ye and Worman , 1994 ) .
They are also probably not phosphorylated during mitosis by p34 `` kinase ( Courvalin et al .
, 1992 ) or targeted to the inner nuclear membrane after synthesis ( Soullam and Worman , 1993 ) .
There is no experimental data to suggest that LBR may function as a sterol reductase , which is the putative function of the yeast ERG24 gene prod-uct , and such a function would not be expected for an inner nuclear membrane protein .
Yeast sts ] * mutants are supersen-sitive to the protein kinase inhibitor staurosporine and also to divalent cations and other drugs ( Shimanuki et al .
, 1992 ) .
This raises the intriguing possibility that the hydrophobic domain of LBR may be involved in the transport of ions or organic compounds between the nucleus and perinuclear space .
Now that the LBR gene and cDNA have been isolated , complementation studies using ERG24 , sts1* , and YGLO22 mutants can be performed to establish if LBR has functions similar to the products of any of these yeast genes .
The present results also provide initial information as to what factors potentially regulate the expression of human LBR .
Analysis of the transcription factor binding sites in the LBR gene promoter suggests that three potential signaling pathways could regulate LBR expression .
The presence of an AP-1 site suggests that LBR transcription may be regulated by phorbol esters ( Angel et al .
, 1987 ) .
The presence of AP-2 sites suggests that expression may be regulated by protein kinase C or cAMP-dependent protein kinase ( Imagawa et al .
, 1987 ) .
A consensus binding sequence for NF-kB is also present in the human LBR gene promoter raising the possibility that LBR expression may be regulated by cytokines ( Lenardo and Balti- Human LBR Gene more , 1989 ) .
Another feature in the LBR gene that could be related to tissue- or development-specific expression is the RNA splicing that occurs in the 5'-untranslated region .
Although we have identified only one transcription start site , the finding of RNA splicing in this region raises the possibility that different transcriptional initiation sites or splice junctions may be used in the 5'-untranslated region to produce mRNAs of various stabilities .
One example of tissue-specific mRNAs with alternative 5'-untranslated sequences is the different transcripts that arise from the « -amylase gene in salivary gland and pancreas ( Young et al .
, 1981 ) .
The presence of three potential polyadenylation signals in the -untranslated region of LBR mRNA also suggests that differentially polyadenylated mRNAs may be used in different cells to produce mRNAs of various stabilities .
Because of its central role in nuclear structure , LBR may have evolved concurrently with the higher eukaryotic nucleus .
The characterization of other genes encoding inner nuclear membrane proteins in diverse species may help to further explain how the complexity of the higher eukaryotic nucleus evolved .
A detailed analysis of LBR gene expression will also demonstrate if a major structural protein of the nuclear envelope is subjected to regulated expression .
Variations in the expression of proteins such as LBR could potentially alter nuclear morphology and genome organization in processes such as cell differentiation , development , and carcinogenesis .
